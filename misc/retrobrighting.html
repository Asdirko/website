Praise The Sun: De-Yellowing ABS Plastics

> Home> Back

The laws of chemistry dictate that over time, polymers oxidize and break down, turning plastics yellow. This is not a rare occurence, and I'm sure that nearly everybody has some firsthand experience or is aware of the phenomenon.

ABS (Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene) plastics are a popular choice for consumer goods as they are cheap, light weight and easily injected molded. It's commmonly used in Lego, computer shells, toys, typewriters, wind instruments and many other consumer goods. ABS is prone to decomposition even in the mildest of enviroments, and ends up turning all manners of sickly yellow and beige colors.

There are many methods that attempt to reverse the effects of yellowing, collectively refered to as "Retrobrighting." While they have different means they all seek the same end: to reverse the chemical reaction caused by the polymers breaking down.

The most popular method involves covering the surface of the ABS with a Hydrogen Peroxide cream (Usually 40 Vol Hair Bleach) and applying UV light for several hours to force the reaction to occur. There are some issues that arise with this method: The materials can be difficult to source, the reaction can leave the the ABS looking streaky and uneven, and the Hydrogen Peroxide can damage the structural integrity of the ABS, causing it to grow brittle or even break.

A couple months before the writing of this article I had bought an original fat Playstation, and it was quite yellowed with age. The internals were in great condition, but the shell had turned an almost greenish-beige. It wasn't the prettiest thing to look at in it's current condition, and I began researching ways to restore it to it's former glory.

I considered the Hydrogen Peroxide method but I wasn't going to risk damaging the shell of the machine, and I didn't want to spend money on a giant UV lamp I would use once. I began researching alternative Retrobrighting methods, and one stood out to me for being suspicously simple.

The solution? Stick your plastics out in the sun for a few days.

It seemed counterintuitive as well. Isn't sunlight that which causes yellowing in the first place? I'm not a chemist, and I don't want to pretend I understand the oxidation process at play here, but I knew I needed to try this method for myself and gather results. After all, at this point my shell couldn't possibly look any worse, and the setup was as easy as placing the shell in a windowsill.

Another bonus from sunlight retrobrightening is that you don't need to dissasemble your electronics to de-yellow them. I already had enough of disasembling my crusty old dualshock the first time I cleaned and serviced it, so it's nice that I don't have to do it again. I stuck a small amount of tape on both my dualshock and the case to serve as a control through the course of the experiment, and into the window they go.
